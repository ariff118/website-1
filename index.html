Welcome

Since June 2015, I have been a post-doc in the (Nano)Materials and Energy group at the Institut Lumière Matière at the University of Lyon, France. I am now working on two different projects involving the application of various types of constraints on low-dimensionality (1D and 2D) systems, in the aim of tuning their electronic, mechanic and magnetic properties. Before that, I performed a two-years post-doc at the Civil and Environmental Engineering department at the Massachussets Institut of Technology (MIT) in Cambridge, Massachussets, working on the reconstruction and simulation of molecular models of amorphous carbons (kerogen). I grew up in Montpellier, in the south of France, and got my Master's degree in Solid State Physics jointly from the Ecole Normale Supérieure in Lyon and from the University Joseph Fourier, in Grenoble. I then obtained my PhD in Physics between the Institut Laue-Langevin (ILL) in Grenoble, and the Laboratoire de Physique des Solides (LPS) in Orsay, France.

Postdoc @ MIT

My work at the MIT was focused on the simulation of disordered nanoporous carbons that are found in sedimentary rocks (gas shale), the so-called "kerogens". These kerogens are the result of the decay of various types of organic matter (marine, terrestrial, lacustrial...) with pressure and temperature, and they are the ones that produce the gas and oil that the industrials try to recover. As kerogen constitutes a minor phase of the shale mixture, they are poorly understood as a stand alone compound.
In order to produce molecular models of these kerogens, I combined experimental measurements of their structure (x-ray and neutron diffraction) with molecular simulation tools such as Hybrid Reverse Monte Carlo and Molecular Dynamics in order to produce atomic structures that match the experimental measurements and are physically and chemically acceptable. These models were then used in other atomistic simulations in order to predict their adsorption and transport properties (Grand Canonical Monte Carlo simualtions), as well as their vibrational or mechanical properties.
In particular, I have shown that the maturity of these kerogen plays an important role on their fracture properties while their composition one influences their elastic properties – this is the subject of an ongoing article.

This work was supervised by Dr. Benoît Coasne and Dr. Roland Pellenq, in the frame of the international CNRS-MIT hub Multi-Scale Materials Science for Energy and Environment, <MSE>2.

PhD Project

My PhD research was focused on the study of the one-dimensional (1D) system that are the carbon nano-peapods. During my work, I primarily used inelastic neutron scattering and x-ray diffraction in order to investigate the structure and dynamics of this system.
At the beggining of my thesis, I spent three months in Tsukuba, Japan, with Dr. Hiromichi Kataura. There, I learnt from the best his method to produce ultra-pure peapods – pure in the neutron sense, i.e. without hydrogen impurities – method that I used to produce large quantities of 2D oriented C60 and C70 peapods.
Thanks to the 2D orientation of the samples, we could separate the translational part of the inelastic response of the spectra, which allowed us to finely study the translational motion of the fullerenes. Then, by studying the inter-fullerene distance, the fullerenes rotation and translations from 2 K up to 1200 K – an unprecedented large temperature domain of stability for 1D systems – I highlited the true crystalline behaviour of the fullerene chains at room temperature, the melting of these chains at high temperature, and the orientational ordering of the fullerenes at low temperature. Quite a lot of phase transitions for a 1D system!

I was supervised by Dr. Stéphane Rols at the Institut Laue Langevin (ILL) in Grenoble, France, and Dr. Pascale Launois at the Laboratoire de Physique des Solides (LPS), in Orsay, France. You can download my PhD dissertation in the Downloads pannel (French version only, sorry!).

Masters Project

My Masters project was focused on the study of the low temperature ordering phase transition in the fullerene-cubane cocrystal. During this project, I performed my first inelastic neutron scattering experiments on IN4 at the ILL, which allowed us to study the phonon density of states of this crystal while cooling down. We identified a mode splitting upon transition in the inter-molecular modes region, and, thanks to the use of Molecular Dynamics, we could link this splitting to a change of environment of the fullerenes which impacted their librations. I am quite proud of the related paper which was selected as an Editor's Suggestion in Phys. Rev. B. (see Publications).

I was supervised by Dr. Stéphane Rols at the Institut Laue Langevin (ILL) in Grenoble, France.

    LAMMPS

    While using LAMMPS, I came to use the AIREBO potential a lot, and came to regret that the Oxygen atoms are not described by this potential. However, there are a couple potentials describing the Oxygen atoms that are available in the litterature, like the one of Ni, Lee and Sinnott or the one of Knippenberg et al.. The latter taking into account the charge equilibration is a priori better than the former, but it is also a lot more difficult to implement in LAMMPS. Therefore, I focused on the first one, much easier to adapt to LAMMPS from the already existing AIREBO code.
    First of all, one need to be able to produce the parameters for the spline interpolations in the right format. So I wrote a piece of code that might come in handy for those who want to play with the various values of the splines in the nodes. It is downloadable here:

    Write AIREBO and OREBO Parameters

    In this zip file, you will find two codes in C (certainly not the most easy way to do it, I agree, but that's what I know...) and the parameter files that they output. One is for airebo with C and H only and reproduces the file that is given in the "potentials" folder of LAMMPS. The second produces the parameter file for the OREBO potential (from Ni, Lee and Sinnott) with the parameters that are given in their publication.
    Once you have the file gathering all the parameters, you need to tell LAMMPS that you know the REBO parameters for another atom. This is done with this updated version of the pair_airebo.cpp file:

    pair_airebo.cpp and pair_airebo.h

    !ATTENTION!
    This version of the pair_airebo.cpp file is compatible with the LAMMPS version of December 13th, 2011. For more recent versions of LAMMPS, you will need to modify it, otherwise there will be issues with the namespaces (at least).
    USE AT YOUR OWN RISKS! I am not a professionnal LAMMPS programmer, there might be bugs, so please proofread before use – and please notify me if you find a bug.

    !ATTENTION AGAIN!
    OREBO does not account for torsion and Lennard-Jones terms. Therefore when using it, use it like you would use AIREBO without these terms, i.e something like:
    units           metal                                  # REBO needs metal
dimension       3                                      # transparent
boundary        p p p                                  # periodic boundaries

read_data       your_file.data                         # read *.data file with the structure

# Define various groups. Attention if no O, remove "3" and "O", etc
group           C           type 1 
group           H           type 2
group           O           type 3

# Define pair interactions. This is with OREBO. Change the folder path to the parameter files "CHO.airebo" according to your own path. 
# /your_path/CHO.airebo C H O means that the atoms of type 1 are C, 2 are H, 3 are O. Change accordingly:
pair_style      airebo 3.0 0 0 # pair_style airebo cutoff LJ Torsion
pair_coeff      * * /your_path/CHO.airebo C H O

    R

    During my thesis I got used to some functions available with the Interactive Data Language (IDL) that I was used to work with because the neutron data treatment program we used, LAMP, was written in IDL. Unfortunately, IDL is quite expensive and I had to look for another language to do my data treatment once I left the ILL.
    And then, I discovered the existence of R.
    R is wonderful, and it's free. It's language is very easy to learn, very similar to IDL or Matlab. I can't recommand enough R, and if you use an awsome text editor like Emacs or Sublime Text 3 (and to use R in ST3, go to this nice tutorial, for example), then you can reach a super optimized workflow when it comes to import and treat your data, produce your figures and compile your LaTeX article, everything in the same text editor.
    Now, when I moved from IDL to R, there was however a few routines that I missed, and I figured I could share them here for people like me (you can also get my full .Rprofile file here):

    reb

    First is the reb(u,N) function that rebins an irregular data set to a regular one by floating average. Below is the code in R, where u is a data.frame with 1, 2 or 3 columns and N is the number of points you want in the regular grid. If u possesses 3 colums, the first one is the x-axis, the second one the measurements and the third one the error on the measure – and the average that is performed is a weighted average.
    reb <- function(u, N){
    M <- dim(u)[1]
    sep <- M / N    
    if(N >= M){
        return("Requested size is longer than initial vector")
    }
    
    if(dim(u)[2] == 1){
        x <- 1:M
        y <- u[,1]
        dy <- rep(1,M)
    }
    if(dim(u)[2] == 2){
        u <- u[order(u[,1]),]
        x <- u[,1]
        y <- u[,2]  
        dy <- rep(1,M)
    }
    if(dim(u)[2] == 3){
        u <- u[order(u[,1]),]
        x <- u[,1]
        y <- u[,2]  
        dy <- u[,3] 
    }
    
    xj <- seq(x[1], x[M], length = N+1 )
    yj <- sapply(1:N, function(i){
            weighted.mean(y[which(x>=xj[(i)] & x<=xj[i+1])],
                         dy[which(x>=xj[(i)] & x<=xj[i+1])])
            })
    dyj <- sapply(1:N, function(i){sqrt(sum((dy[which(x>=xj[(i)] & x<=xj[i+1])])^2))})
    dx <- xj[2] - xj[1]
    xj <- xj[2:(N+1)] - dx / 2
    
    if(dim(u)[2] == 3){
        data.frame(x = xj, y = yj, dy = dyj)
    }else{
        data.frame(x = xj, y = yj)
    }
}
    Example of use: 
    x <- sqrt(1:10000/100)
y <- sin(x)+rnorm(length(x))
d <- data.frame(x,y)
plot(d)
points(reb(d,15),col="red",lwd=4)

    axislog

    If you ever produced a logarithmic plot in R, you might have noticed how ugly the axis are... Here is a function to call instead of axis() to produce regular ticks in the multiples of the powers of ten, with the labels at the powers of ten (with superscripts). 
    axislog <- function(ax,min,max,step=1,labels=TRUE,lwd=1){
    range    <- min:max
    if(labels){label <- sapply(seq(min,max,step),function(i) as.expression(bquote(10^ .(i))))}
    ticksat  <- as.vector(sapply(range, function(p) (1:10)*10^p))
    if(labels){
      if(step==1){axis(ax,at=10^range,labels=label,lwd.ticks=lwd)}
      else{
        axis(ax,at=10^range,labels=FALSE,lwd.ticks=lwd)
        axis(ax,at=10^seq(min,max,step),labels=label,lwd.ticks=lwd)
      }
    }
    else{axis(ax,at=10^range,labels=FALSE,lwd.ticks=lwd)}
    axis(ax, ticksat, labels=NA, tcl=0.25, lwd=0, lwd.ticks=lwd)
}
    Example of use: 
    x <- 1:10000*1e-2
y <- sqrt(x)
par(mgp = c(2.1, 0.8, 0), tck = 0.02, lwd = 2)
# Ugly plot
plot(x,y,log="x",axes=F,xlim=c(1e-2,100))
axis(1)
box()
# Nice plot
plot(x,y,log="x",axes=F,xlim=c(1e-2,100))
axislog(1,-2,2)
axislog(3,-2,2,step=2)
box()
